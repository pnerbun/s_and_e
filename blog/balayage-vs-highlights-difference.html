Balayage vs. Highlights: What's the Difference?

The difference between balayage and highlights comes down to how the color is applied. Balayage is hand-painted freehand onto the surface of your hair for a soft, sun-kissed gradient with no harsh regrowth line. Highlights are woven sections saturated from the root and wrapped in foil, giving brighter, more uniform lift.

You've saved a dozen photos to the same Pinterest board, and half of them say "balayage" while the other half say "highlights" — and honestly, some of them look pretty similar. So which one do you actually ask for when you sit down in the chair?

It's the most common color question we hear, and the good news is there's no wrong answer — just a right answer for you. The difference between balayage and highlights changes how your color grows out, how often you'll come back, and what you'll spend over a year. Here's how to tell them apart and choose with confidence.

Balayage vs. Highlights: The Core Difference

Both techniques lighten your hair — they just get there differently.

Balayage (from the French for "to sweep") is painted on by hand. Your stylist sweeps lightener onto the surface of selected sections, concentrating it through the mid-lengths and ends and keeping it soft near the roots. The result is a natural, blended gradient — brighter on the bottom, deeper up top, like your hair spent a summer at the lake.

Highlights are more structured. Thin sections are woven out with a comb, saturated with lightener from root to tip, and folded into foils. The foil traps heat and lifts each section evenly, so you get consistent, all-over brightness with more contrast.

The short version: balayage is soft and sun-kissed; highlights are bright and uniform.

How Each Technique Is Applied

The application is really where these two part ways.

  • Balayage is freehand. No foils, no set pattern. Because your stylist places every stroke by eye, balayage is customizable to your face shape, your part, and how you wear your hair.
  • Highlights are foiled. The foil packets and the woven, root-to-tip saturation give you that bright, even lift — exactly what you want if you're starting from darker hair or you love a lot of contrast.

You'll also hear about partial vs. full balayage. A partial focuses the painting around your face and part — the pieces that frame your face and show up most in photos — while a full balayage works all the way around the head for brightness from every angle. If you're new to lighter hair, a partial is a lovely, lower-commitment way to start.

How Often You'll Need a Touch-Up

This is the part that quietly matters most for your calendar and your wallet.

Because balayage doesn't start at the root, there's no sharp line of regrowth to chase. Most people comfortably stretch balayage to every three to four months (about 12–16 weeks). So how often should you get balayage? Roughly three or four times a year for most heads of hair.

Highlights, with lightened sections beginning right at the scalp, show natural regrowth within four to six weeks, and most people rebook every six to ten weeks to keep that root-to-end brightness looking intentional.

A quick tip: a toner appointment in between can buy you extra weeks. A toner is a fast, gentle gloss that neutralizes brassiness and refreshes your tone without re-lightening — so your color stays cool and expensive-looking while your grow-out relaxes.

The Real Cost — Per Visit vs. Over a Year

Here's where we'll be honest with you, because this is where a lot of color guides quietly mislead: balayage usually costs more per visit than a partial highlight. It's a freehand, time-intensive service, and the price reflects that.

But how much does balayage cost over a whole year is the better question. Because you're only in the chair three or four times a year instead of six or eight, the annual math often evens out — and sometimes tips in balayage's favor. Fewer visits, less time off work, and a grow-out you don't have to panic about.

At Smith + Elliot, balayage starts at $135, foils start at $50, and a toner starts at $30. Final pricing always depends on your hair's length, density, and where you're starting from — which is exactly what a quick consultation is for. We'd rather quote you honestly in person than surprise you at the front desk.

Which One Is Right for You?

Choose balayage if you:

  • Want a soft, natural, grown-out look
  • Would rather visit the salon a few times a year than every month
  • Like the idea of low-maintenance color that ages gracefully

Choose highlights if you:

  • Want brighter, more uniform lift or lots of contrast
  • Are starting from darker hair that needs foils to really go light
  • Don't mind more frequent touch-ups to keep things crisp

And if you can't decide? Plenty of our favorite results use a little of both — foils up top for brightness, hand-painting through the ends for softness. That's the kind of thing worth talking through in a consultation.

Making Your Color Last

Whichever you choose, a few habits go a long way toward how to make balayage last longer (and highlights, too):

  1. Wait 48 hours before your first wash so the color fully settles.
  2. Wash less often — two to three times a week — and reach for dry shampoo in between.
  3. Go sulfate-free. Sulfates strip color faster; a gentle, color-safe shampoo keeps it vibrant.
  4. Rinse cool. Cooler water helps seal the cuticle and lock tone in.
  5. Deep condition weekly and use a heat protectant every time you style. Lightened hair is thirstier hair.
  6. Book a toner around the 6–8 week mark to refresh your tone and stretch the time between full appointments.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is balayage or highlights cheaper?

Per visit, a partial highlight is usually cheaper than balayage. Over a year, balayage can cost about the same or less because you need fewer appointments to maintain it.

Which lasts longer, balayage or highlights?

Balayage lasts longer between appointments — typically 12 to 16 weeks — because there's no obvious regrowth line. Highlights usually need a touch-up every 6 to 10 weeks.

Is balayage better for dark hair?

Balayage looks beautiful on dark hair for a soft, sun-kissed effect, but if you want dramatic lightening or lots of brightness, foil highlights (or a combination) may get you there faster.

What is a toner and do I need one?

A toner is a quick, gentle gloss that neutralizes brassy or warm tones and refreshes your color without re-lightening. Most color clients love a toner between appointments to keep their shade cool and fresh.

Does balayage damage your hair?

Because balayage is painted on the surface and usually kept off the roots, it's often gentler than all-over foils. Any lightening service can stress the hair, so aftercare and the occasional deep-conditioning treatment matter.

How do I know which one to book?

Start with a consultation. Bring a few inspiration photos, and your stylist will tell you honestly whether balayage, highlights, or a mix will get you there — and what it'll take to maintain.

The Bottom Line

Balayage and highlights are both gorgeous — they just get you to different places. Balayage is soft, low-maintenance, and grows out gracefully; highlights are bright, uniform, and crisp. The best way to choose is to talk it through with a stylist who'll be straight with you about upkeep and cost.
